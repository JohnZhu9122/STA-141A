We will use the Fatalities dataset from the AER package, which contains annual, state-level information on traffic fatalities and related variables in the United States. Your first task is to aggregate the data across time. Specifically, load the Fatalities dataset, and for each state compute the average number of traffic fatalities across all available years in the dataset. Then, create a heatmap (choropleth map) of the continental United States in which each state is colored according to its average annual number of traffic fatalities. You may use built-in U.S. map data available in R from map_data("state"). Your map should include a clear title and legend. Finally, write 24 sentences describing any spatial patterns you observe in the map, such as regional differences or clustering of high or low fatality levels.

Note: This is a challenging question that is not covered in lectures.

In[10]:
# 5
library(AER)
library(dplyr)
library(ggplot2)
library(maps)
library(mapproj)

data("Fatalities")

mean_fatalities <- aggregate(
  fatal ~ state,
  data = Fatalities, 
  FUN = mean)

check_states <- data.frame(
    state = tolower(state.abb), 
    region = tolower(state.name)
    )

complete_data <- merge(
  mean_fatalities, 
  check_states, 
  by = "state"
  )

us_map <- map_data("state")

final_data <- left_join(us_map, complete_data, by = "region")

ggplot(final_data, aes(x = long, y = lat, group = group,fill = fatal)) +
  geom_polygon(color = "white") +
  labs(
    title = "Average Annual Motor Vehicle Fatalities by State",
    fill = "Avg Fatalities"
  ) +
  coord_map()

In this plot, it shows that the traffic fatalities has a strong relationship between states' population. Califorina, Texas and Florida have the most three population, and three of them all have the highest traffic fatalities. And in the Wyoming, because its population is the smallest so that its traffic fatalities is lowest.
